# What "good" looks like here

We've earned it. Three episodes of reflection — the map, the anchors, the blast radius — and we never once said *optimisation*. We catalogued the drift and the shadow IT in the language of consequences, in front of the people who built the estate, and they trusted us with it. That trust is the asset, and now we get to spend it.

But not yet on fixes. On a definition.

Because "optimisation" is meaningless until you've answered *optimise toward what?* And the answer is never the generic best-practice checklist you could copy off a Microsoft docs page. The answer is **this estate's own definition of good** — specific to this team, these constraints, this risk appetite. Defining it is the bridge from reflection to the Engine, and it produces the single most useful artefact of the whole engagement: a number.

## A number they can show upward

> **The Capability Baseline** — a scored maturity assessment across a small number of axes. Gives the team a number to improve and something to show their own executives.

Five axes. Each scored 0–5, from the reflection — metadata only, no new data gathering. Here is Awatea Regional Health, as we found it:

The scorecard above is doing three jobs at once, and they all matter.

**It's honest without being cruel.** Every score has a *why* attached, drawn straight from what we already showed them. "Semantic governance: 1.5 — one of four models certified; 'Total Patients' defined three ways." That's not a new accusation. It's the drift from Episode 1 and the certification gaps from the map, given a coordinate. They've already seen all of it. The number just totals it.

**It's a baseline, not a verdict.** The word is deliberate. A verdict is a judgement you pass on someone. A baseline is a starting line you measure *progress* from. "1.4 overall" isn't "you're failing" — it's "this is where the development wheel starts, and in six months you'll show your executive a bigger number, with our help." Reframing the same score from judgement to trajectory is the difference between a defensive room and a motivated one.

**It's defensible internally.** This is the one people underrate. The official who brought us in has to justify the spend to *their* boss. "We hired a consultant and they said nice things" doesn't survive a budget review. "We established a capability baseline of 1.4 across five governance axes, here's the roadmap to 3.0, here's the quarter-by-quarter target" survives anything. The baseline is the artefact that lets our champion defend us when we're not in the room.

## Why these five axes

The axes aren't arbitrary, and they aren't generic. Each one is the measurable shadow of a concept from earlier:

| Axis | What it measures | Where you saw it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Semantic governance** | certification, single definitions | the three "Total Patients" |
| **Lineage visibility** | can you trace report → source | the undocumented dependencies |
| **Reuse / conformance** | shared dimensions and measures | every model redefining its own logic |
| **Performance posture** | model size, refresh health | the 2.1 GB, 73-minute Workforce model |
| **Shadow-IT exposure** | ungoverned load-bearing assets | `Bed_Capacity_Manual.xlsx` |

A team can argue with an adjective. They cannot argue with "one of four models is certified," because we showed them the map. The baseline works precisely because reflection came first — every score is a receipt for something already on screen.

## "Good" is the gap, not the ceiling

Here's the move that turns a baseline into a strategy. You don't define "good" as 5/5 on every axis. A regional health authority does not need, and should not pay for, a perfect score on reuse. **Good is the specific gap that, if closed, removes the most risk for the least disruption** — for *this* estate.

Look at the two 1.0s. Reuse/conformance and shadow-IT exposure are tied for lowest, but they are not equally urgent. Shadow-IT exposure is a 1.0 *and* it's the axis whose failure mode is "the board sees wrong numbers." Reuse is a 1.0 whose failure mode is "engineers duplicate work." Both are real. Only one of them is a phone call from the CFO.

So "good," for Awatea, starts as: *get shadow-IT exposure off the floor first* — replace the load-bearing spreadsheet with a governed bed-capacity dimension — *then* attack reuse by giving them shared, conformed assets. The baseline didn't just measure the estate; it **sequenced the work.**

## This is the hinge of the whole method

Step back and look at the shape of the series so far:

- Episodes 0–2 were **reflection**: understand, map, trace — build trust, change nothing.
- This episode is the **hinge**: convert understanding into *this estate's* definition of good, expressed as a baseline that sequences the work.
- Episodes 4 onward are **the Engine**: acceleration — and now every fix we make has a coordinate to move and a reason to be first.

That split mirrors the product exactly. The method is open: anyone can learn to run a reflection and score a baseline. The Engine is what runs it continuously, sharpens the scoring across every estate it sees, and keeps the number climbing without the team owning the R&D.
